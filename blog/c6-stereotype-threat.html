The Incidious Cycle

Reducing Stereotype Threat

12/4/14

Every culture, race, nation, sexual orientation, gender, age, religion etc., has their own slew of negative stereotypes but until it was brought into the light for me at Dev Bootcamp I never really had a notion of how insidious stereotype threat can be.

Stereotype threat is described as the phenomena that happens when one feels that, through their performance, either academically or physically, they may perpetuate or confirm a stereotype associated with their identity. It has been shown that when one faces this pressure while taking tests it often serves to distract or stress a given person which in turn causes a drop in performance that is not seen when stereotype threat is removed from the equation. This causes a vicious cycles and perpetrates stereotypes both to those being subject to them and to those observing the results.

Though I have been subject to a few stereotypes I can't claim I have ever felt disparaged by any, and after reading so much about how significantly this effects so many people my experiences seems rather irrelevant. However, since I have been prompted to, I will mention a couple. I think the times that I feel it the most is when I find myself in a discussion that has been sensationalized to be about race or sexism but may have many more facets that are often overlooked. Generally in these discussions I immediately (and correctly) assume that I have far less experience with the topic and tend to take a more passive stance listening to the discussion. However on the occasion that I do point out that this issue is not so one dimensional, I often feel that my opinion, whatever it may be, is either rendered inert because I am a white male, or I am labeled as a racist, sexist, supremacist; the enemy. Certainly this is not always the case but it has happened frequently enough for me to chose not to get involved. In this situation I don't feel like there would be any winning in me getting involved and facing the stereotype threat. They have made up their mind to believe what they believe and I would only get roped into a shouting match so instead I listen or walk away. In less extreme examples when I do chose to participate in the discussion I just need to be careful to clearly express my sentiment and not step on any toes.

Now that I have an understanding of what stereotype threat is I feel I will be able to more effectively identify when I am feeling it and when others in my group are feeling it as well. I think a great way to curb this threat is to discuss, in a group, our experience with the material or the test. This way everyone can see that they were not the only ones who felt a certain way making them feel that any given social identity has far less to do with their current experience.
